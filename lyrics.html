Looking back at the top Hip-Hop artist of 2018 on genius

By Ken Partridge

News

Sep 22, 2023

Drake remains too big to fail.

In honor of hip-hop's 50th anniversary year, we're looking back at the top artists, songs, albums, and producers of “The Genius Era,” 2009 to the present.

Theoretically, things could've taken a turn for Drake in 2018. As the new year dawned, the Canadian superstar had been on top of the rap game for nearly a decade, and each of his last seven projects—four albums and three mixtapes—had reached No. 1 on the Billboard 200. That kind of longevity is rare in pop music and virtually unheard of in hip-hop, so on some level, Drizzy was due for a drop-off. And then there was the massive L he took in May of 2018, when longtime rival Pusha T decisively won their beef by revealing to the world in “The Story of Adidon” that Drake was “hiding a child” he'd fathered out of wedlock.

But neither the march of time nor Pusha's bombshell did much to slow Drake's momentum. The cocksure Canadian had himself another very successful 2018, and looking over his list of accomplishments, it's no surprise that he stands as the top hip-hop artist of that year on Genius according to pageviews.

Drake's first major move in 2018 was releasing the two-song Scary Hours EP, featuring the bouncy single “Go's Plan,” in which Drizzy makes his fame and fortune sound like an act of divine will. It's not exactly a song of gratitude, though he does shoutout “the broskies”—producer Noah “40” Shebib and manager Oliver El-Khatib—for helping him build a career he couldn't have made on his own. In perhaps the most quoted line of the song, Drake once again embodies the cuddly playboy persona that's long been his shtick. He makes no apology for being emotionally unavailable to the women he beds, yet he expects we'll still find him sweet and lovable because he's such a mama's boy.

She say, “Do you love me?” I tell her, “Only partly I only love my bed and my mama, I'm sorry”

“God's Plan” debuted at No. 1 on the Billboard Hot 100, giving Drake his first solo chart-topper, and it broke a record for on-demand first-week streams. The in early February, Drake guested on Memphis rapper BlocBoy JB's “Look Alive,” and a month later, he turned up on Migos' “Walk It Talk It.” The latter became Drake's 70th Top 40 hit on the Hot 100, and when both songs reached their impressive chart peaks, Drake surpassed JAY-Z to become the rapper with the most all-time Top 10 hits (23) on the Hot 100.

The good times continued into April, as a new Drake single, “Nice for What,” debuted atop the Hot 100, replacing “God's Plan,” which had commanded the summit for 11 weeks. Then came May and the release of Pusha T's DAYTONA, a Kanye West-produced album that concludes with “Infrared,” a song loaded with some subliminal Drake disses that weren't lost on the public. The chief criticism leveled in Pusha's song is that Drake uses ghostwriters, and Drizzy responded hours later with “Duppy Freestyle,” in which he deflects the accusation as only he can.

Whoever supposedly makin' me hits But then got no hits sound like they need me My hooks did it, my lyrics did it My spirit did it, I'm fearless with it, yeah

Pusha responded with the nuclear option. On “The Story of Adidon,” which flips the beat from JAY-Z's “The Story of O.J.,” Pusha reveals that Drake has a son, Adonis, and that he'd been planning on introducing the youngster to the world via an Adidas campaign. To make matters worse, the cover art is an image of Drake in blackface taken by photographer David Leyes years earlier. It was “one of the most vicious, merciless takedowns in rap history,” according to Stereogum critic Tom Breihan, and everyone wondered how in the hell Drake was going to strike back.

Except he didn't really need to. In June, Drake dropped his eagerly anticipated fifth album, Scorpion, a ridiculously long and lush-sounding double LP that addresses, among other things, his anxieties about being a single father and his reasons for keeping Adonis a secret. On the song “Emotionless,” featuring a sample of the club remix of Mariah Carey's 1991 classic “Emotions,” Drake completely flips Pusha's “deadbeat dad” narrative and paints himself as a caring father acting purely out of concern for his boy's wellbeing.

I wasn't hiding my kid from the world I was hiding the world from my kid From empty souls who just wake up and look to debate Until you staring at your seed, you can never relate

Scorpion concludes with “March 14,” named for the day in which Drake learned via DNA test that French artist and former adult film star Sophia Brussaux was pregnant with his child. In the song, Drake is frank about how awful he feels about the whole situation. As the product of a broken home, he always thought he'd do better with his own kids.

It's breaking my spirit Single father, I hate when I hear it I used to challenge my parents on every album Now I'm embarrassed to tell 'em I ended up as a co-parent

In addition to being a PR win, Scorpion was a super-mega blockbuster. It set single-day streaming records on Spotify and Apple Music, and all 25 tracks made the Billboard Hot 100. And because “Look Alive” and Lil Baby's Drake-assisted “Yes Indeed” were still on the charts, Drake found himself with 27 songs on the Hot 100, a new record. Seven of those tunes made the Top 10, giving Drake 31 over the course of his career and making him the male solo artist with the most Top 10 pop hits. (The artist he surpassed? Michael Jackson, who appears posthumously on the Scorpion track “Don't Matter to Me.”)

Among those Top 10 hits was another No. 1, “In My Feelings,” which spawned a viral (and potentially dangerous) dance challenge and kept Drake in the headlines throughout July 2018. The following month, Drake turned up uncredited on Travis Scott's “Sicko Mode,” which topped the Hot 100 and became one of the year's biggest rap hits. Drake is feeling celebratory on the chorus, his umpteenth opportunity to brag about the staggering distance between where he came from (public transportation) and where he now resides (public jets).

She's in love with who I am Back in high school, I used to bus it to the dance (Yeah) Now I hit the FBO with duffels in my hands

And Drake still wasn't done. In August, he teamed with hip-hop's then-hottest group for the four-month Aubrey & the Three Migos Tour, and in October, he showed off his Spanish skills on Bad Bunny's “MIA,” which gave Drake one last Top 5 hit to end the year. In November, Drake announced plans to take time off and focus on producing movies and TV shows, and sure enough, in 2019, he became executive producer of HBO's Euphoria and Netflix's Top Boy.

Drake may not appear again in this column as the top hip-hop artist of any other year on Genius. But between 2011 and 2018, he earned the honor four times: 2011, 2013, 2015, and 2018. While he and Kendrick Lamar might legitimately argue about which artist dominated hip-hop in the 2010s, The Guardian had it spot on when it declared Drake the “definitive pop star of his generation.”

Here are the Top 10 hip-hop artists of 2018 on Genius.

  1. Drake
  2. Eminem
  3. Travis Scott
  4. XXXTENTACION
  5. 6ix9ine
  6. Kanye West
  7. Post Malone
  8. YoungBoy Never Broke Again
  9. J.Cole
  10. Nicki Minaj
